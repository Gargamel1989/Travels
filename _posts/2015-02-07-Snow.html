Let's start this one off with some sad news.

I broke up with my bike... After trying my hardest to make things work, I realized there were just too many scars left from previous relationships for this to go on. We had our moments, but honestly most of the time it felt like an uphill battle. Some things were said that shouldn't have been said; she told me I was afraid of commitment, I told her she was just a shitty bike. All in all I learned a lot from our relationship, but from now on I'll think twice before jumping into something serious before getting to know each other a bit better. I'll still miss her on the downhill though...

To Morvan

While planning the next part of my trip, my sights had fallen on the Parc Naturel Régional du Morvan. It was conveniently located between my location and Dijon, which was my next big city to visit, and according to google it boasted some fine nature. My kind of thing!

The nearest train station that wasn't Fontainebleau (I developed a disliking for backtracking, thanks Bram!), was located in Montargis, only 30km away! Time for a little walk before the big walk.

I took a small break in Cépoy, right before Montargis, as this village was home to the last hostel I would encounter until my arrival in Dijon. A lot of my gear was wet, my legs were pretty tired from climbing and walking and a first blister was forming on my right heel.

I took the train from Montargis to Nevers and the bus from Nevers to Chateau-Chinon, a large village at the edge of the parc where a lot of trails started. The trip took longer than expected and by the time I got there it was 22h00 and raining quite heavily. I had to find a sheltered place to sleep quickly. This led to probably the weirdest place I have ever slept in my life. I can now say I have literally slept on a pile of (synthetic) shit. Hooray!

{% include img.html url='img/snow/d4-03_sleepingspot' %} A massive sheltered storage of synthetic fertilizer near the station. Very soft and cozy, no smell at all and most important: dry. Actually a great sleeping spot.

Thankfully, my luck turned in the morning when I found this bad boy not 3 minutes from my camping spot

Day 3: Back to reality (kind of)

I woke up very early because I had to walk 18km today and wanted to arrive at my destination as early as possible to increase my chances of catching a bus to the nearest town with a train station.

My first few kilometers were walked in the dark of night, which was a cool experience but maybe not the smartest thing ever :). Luckily I didn't lose the trail.

{% include img.html url='img/snow/d7-01_earlywalking' %} Sun is starting to break through.
{% include img.html url='img/snow/d7-02_selfie' %} Oh look, it's me.
{% include img.html url='img/snow/d7-03_riverroad' %} I thought I was descending again, since the snow seamed to be melting.
{% include img.html url='img/snow/d7-04_forest' %} But actually I was ascending and the snow only got deeper and deeper (up to 40cm).

Some 8km before the end of my trail, I was starting to feel like: "Well, this has been fun but I've about had my share of snow for this week now". The trail felt differently however, and threw this in my face:

{% include img.html url='img/snow/d7-05_branches' %} Overhanging branches due to the weight of the snow blocking the trail.

This kept going for about 1km. Definitely one of the most enjoyable parts of the journey! Luckily I had my trusty walking stick to hit the branches, which helped a bit but was mostly just good fun because it made me feel like a wizard. The only real way to get through was a combination of forcefully swinging the branches and at a certain point just bombing through without thinking about how wet, cold and hurt you're getting.

{% include img.html url='img/snow/d7-06_sticklove' %} Give the stick some love.

During the last kilometers my trail joined the main road, and I got picked up by a nice lady with a baking truck who wondered what I was doing walking in the snow. She gave me a lift to Anost and informed me that there were no busses on sunday. Great...

{% include img.html url='img/snow/d7-07_walkingstick' %} Had to say goodbye... I'll always remember you.

The nearest train station was located in Autun, 24km down the road. Without busses I had no other option but to start walking and stick my thumb out. After 6 kilometers a very nice local guy drove me all the way there even though he was actually just on his way home, a few hundred meters from where he picked me up. He also gave me a little tourist information about Autun which turned out to be a surprisingly interesting town. Since everything went faster than expected I had 2 hours before my train to Dijon left, so I decided to explore the town a little bit

Appendix: The evolution of a blister (NSFL) show

So as mention before, after my prelude walk from La Chapelle La Reine to Cépoy, I developed my first real blister on my heel (climbing blisters don't count). Back then it was such a cuty:

{% include img.html url='img/snow/blister_cepoy' %}

I wasn't too worried, just slapped a big bad Compeed on there and pretty much forgot about it. Apparently walking for 3 days in pretty much constantly soggy boots is bad for blister recovery.. Who would have known...

So when I came out of the woods, I took a little peak:

{% include img.html url='img/snow/blister_dijon' %}

As you can see (or maybe not), my foot is still a bit soaked here, and it is hard to see where the blister ends and my skin begins. It's not very distinguishable in the picture, but at this point it looked as if the entire bottom of my heel was a blister.

When I arrived in Dijon I popped it, drained it and let it dry for a while. Here is the result:

{% include img.html url='img/snow/blister_after' %}

Doesn't look so bad anymore... Still easily the biggest blister I've ever had, but not nearly as bad as I expected when the Compeed came off :).

Lessons Learned:

  • I really, really love snow and the sound of it cracking beneath my feet. I don't think I would have enjoyed this walk nearly as much if I didn't.
  • Regina has her limits.
  • Don't look at blisters right after they come out of your wet shoes
  • Lift givers are the nicest people
