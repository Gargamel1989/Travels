After my first night there, I decided to stay in Reims for another day to explore the city and tinker with my bike. The saddle had been sliding down during the last 2 days and by now I was practically riding a childrens bike.

Reims turned out to be a fairly nice city with some beautiful buildings, the most notable of course being the cathedral.

{% preview img/champagne/01-reims-cat.jpg %} The only angle that didn't include ugly scaffolding

The other side of the city, which is just a 1-minute bikeride from the center, was a bit less impressive. Most of the streets and buildings had decayed quite heavily and the people seemed pretty unhappy in general. Les banlieues? It got me thinking about wealth and freedom and made me a bit sad. Heavy stuff!

On a more positive note, I made my first travel buddy on my second night in Reims. An extremely helpful French guy who gave me my first real conversation since starting this journey, some sound travel advice and lended me a bunch of tools he had lying about in his car so I could fix my bike (didn't work, hurt my thumb). Also, for reasons unknown, he assumed I did not know what a bundling rope was and went to great heights to explain the intricate details of this novel invention.

{% preview img/champagne/02-phuc.jpg %} Phuc, I was tired

Next morning was the start of my second attempt to test my multi-day hiking skills. The plan was to hike/bike from Reims to the city of Meaux over the course of 3 days. From there it would be relatively cheap to take the train to Paris. Sounded great!

Day 1 was beautiful, but unfortunately mostly covered in mist (apparently the French weather guys are just as bad at predicting the weather). After about 1 hour of biking the Google Maps routeplanner decided to fuck me over and stop working, so I had to improvise and take a different route. This ended up being a slight detour, but probably way more scenic than my original route.

By the time I reached my endpoint for the day, Mareuil-le-Port, it had been dark for a good 30 minutes, my shoulders and upper back were completely blocked an my right knee hurt quite a bit. My body sucks... I decided to hit up the local drug dealer and get some of the good stuff*, hoping the pain would be gone by the next morning. I spent my first night with Regina (which is how I have decided to name my tent) in a small public park right next to the Marne.

Day 2 started with some drizzle and mist, only slightly less pain than the evening before and an epic wrong turn which led me through some pretty muddy fields (which fucked up my bike even more). This day was also quite boring as far as views go.

The pain in my knees and back had again been steadily increasing throughout the day and by the end were accompanied by a slight fever, yet somehow I still managed to arrive in Charly-sur-Marne before sunset. After wasting 2 more hours waiting for the darkness I found another small park on the outskirts of town and checked out for the day.

At around 3 in the morning, my night's rest was interrupted by what sounded like someone firing a gun right next to me. I was scared like a little bitch, very tired and it was cold outside, so I decided against taking a peak and went back to sleep. The next morning did bring me an explanation:

{% preview img/champagne/15-tire.jpg %} This bike man...

With the help of a friendly bypasser and his tools, changing the tire only took me the better part of an hour. After 2 more hours of uphill walking, which by this time would better classified as limping, I came by the sign that meant the end of my woes.

{% preview img/champagne/16-end.jpg %} Cheap-ass trains, here I come

Since trains within the ÃŽle de France region are cheap, I decided the long-term survival of my knees was more important than my ego and headed over to the nearest station instead of continuing to Meaux (still 20km left). After an hour of feeling like a massive bum taking a train with the shittiest and dirtiest of bikes, I finally arrived in the City of Love.

So the results of this second test:

  • Wrecked my right knee (unable to put my full body weight + backpack on it)
  • A medicated fever (I assumed this was a fatigue fever)
  • The inability to look sideways due to upper back pains
  • Mentally and physically exhausted
  • Didn't achieve my goal

This is going great! Why didn't anybody tell me this was going to be hard?

* Ibuprofen

Lessons Learned:

  • A bundling rope is a device to tie stuff to your bike's baggage rack. You attach it to the iron platelets on either side of the bike using the hooks and put your stuff under the rope.
  • Google Maps is not to be trusted.
  • More extensive testing of the bike is required before embarking on such a journey.
  • Sleeping in a tent for multiple days without having a place to relax for a bit is no fun
